Intro to Reinforcement Learning

Hello Everyone 😀 Welcome to this first post ! Today we are going to talk about Reinforcement Learning (RL). This post is a beginner friendly introduction to RL. I will explain what is RL, why RL, what are some of the most important challenges in the fields of RL and some common applications of RL.

Introduction

Animals, including humans, learn by interacting with their environments and they do so continuously. For instance, when an infant plays, waves its arms or looks about, he receives tons of information about the world thanks to his senses. He learns about the causality i.e. causes and consequences of his actions. He tries things and learns about himself and the environment from that experience. In order to learn how to solve a new task, he must do lot of trial-and-errors depending on the complexity and difficulty of the task at hands, without necessarily relying on any teacher. The purpose of Reinforcement Learning is to provide computational methods to learn from experience. RL is thus learning what to do, how to map situations to actions, by maximizing a numerical reward signal. The promise of RL is to allow machines to learn as any other animal by interaction with their environment and continuously. An RL agent interacting within an environment does not know the causality in that environment i.e. it is not told which actions yield the most reward but must discover that by trying them. Some of the most distinguishing characteristic of RL is that the actions of the RL agent may affect not only the immediate reward but also the next situations and, through that, all subsequent rewards. The term "situations" could be replaced by "states" or "observations". These three terms are similar in practice and represent whatever information that are useful to a learning agent to solve a given task. For example, in a game of chess the state is the chess board with all current pieces on it. In a robotic navigation, the state may be the robot velocity, position, orientation, etc. In fact, each of these quantities can constitute a state itself, but also their combination can constitute a unique state. Note that, for this late example, the state of the world or the environment is not fully known unlike in the game of chess because we have decided to select some aspects of the world and by selecting those we discarded others, like visual information.
